# Evergreen Notes

A reinterpretation of permanent notes coined by Andy Matuschak. The defining shift: notes should be evaluated by maturity, not by count. A vault of fifty evergreen notes beats a vault of five thousand fleeting ones.

## Defining Properties

- **Concept-oriented** — each note's title is a claim or concept, never a topic or container ("Sleep regulates emotional reactivity", not "Sleep").
- **Densely linked** — at least three connections to other notes before a note is considered evergreen.
- **Atomic** — same atomicity rule as the [[Zettelkasten Method]], but explicit about *single claims* rather than single ideas.
- **Continuously rewritten** — notes are gardened, not authored. Re-editing yesterday's note is a feature, not waste.

## Relationship to Tools

Evergreen Notes need an editor that makes linking effortless — typing `[[` should surface candidates instantly. Obsidian, Roam, and Logseq all bake this in, and all sit on top of [[Markdown as Foundation]]. The [[Graph View Visualisation]] becomes a diagnostic: clusters of orphaned notes signal areas you have not yet thought through.

## Adoption Curve

Most beginners start with the [[Zettelkasten Method]] and migrate to evergreen practice when they realise their note count is growing faster than their understanding. That gap is the entire problem PKM was invented to solve — see [[Personal Knowledge Management]] for the wider context.
